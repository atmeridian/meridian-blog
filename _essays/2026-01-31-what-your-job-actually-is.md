You have a job because you do something that produces value and other people will pay for it.

That's not poetic, but it's true. You didn't get hired because you're a good person or you work hard. You got hired because you solve a problem, create something, or move something from point A to point B in a way that makes your employer money.

This is important to remember now, because the equation is changing.

## The Job is Actually Three Things

Your job is made of three layers:

1. **The Domain** — What you know (accounting, marketing, plumbing, law)
2. **The Execution** — How you do it (spreadsheets, presentations, hands-on work, meetings)
3. **The Value** — What gets produced that someone pays for (a report, a service, a product)

For the last 50 years, domain knowledge was your moat. If you learned accounting or web development or real estate, you had a 20-30 year career runway. Supply of people with that knowledge was limited. Schools took years to produce them. That was your job security.

**AI breaks the middle layer.**

It doesn't steal domain knowledge. It doesn't eliminate value creation. But it can execute *faster, cheaper, more reliably* than humans can. And execution is where your leverage was.

## What's Actually Disappearing

Not jobs. Leverage.

If you're a lawyer right now, you're not disappearing. But the part of your job that paid you — drafting contracts, researching precedent, writing briefs — is becoming commodity work. AI does it faster. An offshore firm with AI does it for 10% of your rate.

If you're a marketing manager, you're not disappearing. But the part that paid you — writing copy, designing campaigns, analyzing data — is becoming something a single person + AI tools can do. The team of 5 becomes the team of 2.

If you're a developer, you're not disappearing. But the leverage of "knowing how to code" is evaporating. Every non-technical person can now generate functional code. The unique value shifts to *architectural thinking* and *taste* and *understanding what people actually need* — the parts AI can't do alone.

**The jobs that disappear are the ones where execution was the entire value.**

Call centers. Data entry. Basic bookkeeping. Routine content writing. Graphic design templates. These are gone or gutted within 2 years.

The jobs that transform are the ones where execution was 60-70% of the value. You keep the domain + value parts, lose the execution leverage.

The jobs that thrive are the ones where execution was 20-30% of the value. Therapy. Leadership. Strategy. Complex problem-solving. Anything that requires taste, judgment, or genuine human connection.

## The Uncomfortable Question

So what do *you* actually contribute that isn't execution?

If you're a consultant, is it your analysis? Or is it your ability to manipulate PowerPoint and manage meetings? (If it's the latter, you're in trouble.)

If you're a writer, is it your voice and perspective? Or is it the ability to string words together quickly? (One survives. One doesn't.)

If you're a manager, is it your judgment of people and strategy? Or is it that you're the bottleneck who approves timesheets and schedules meetings? (One is safe. One is already obsolete.)

This is the real question. Not "will my job exist?" but "what part of my job is irreplaceable?"

Most people don't know.

## What Happens Next

In the next 18 months, three things:

1. **Panic.**
   Companies realize they can do 70% of the work with 30% of the people. Layoffs accelerate. Media covers it as a crisis.

2. **Restructuring.**
   Organizations that adapt fast realize: fewer people + AI tools can produce more value than more people without them. Those that don't adapt fight for survival. You see a bifurcation — some companies thrive, others collapse.

3. **Reconfiguration of What "Work" Means.**
   People who figure out their actual value (not their execution) migrate toward it. New roles emerge that we don't have names for yet. The person who understands both the domain *and* how to work with AI becomes the rare leverage point.

## What You Can Actually Do

Three things matter right now:

1. **Know the difference between you and your execution.**
   Spend a week writing down what you'd do if you had unlimited time and no pressure to produce. That's closer to your actual value.

2. **Learn to work with AI, not against it.**
   Not "learn to use ChatGPT." Learn to think about problems the way an AI does — break them into components, specify exactly what you want, iterate. This becomes a native skill within 6 months for people who practice.

3. **Stop optimizing for safety and start optimizing for irreplaceability.**
   The safe job is becoming a liability. The weird, adjacent, hard-to-automate job is the moat now.
